The most dangerous AI claim is the one that is mostly true

June 2026

AI vendors don't lie to you. They show you the best case and let you assume it's the typical case. That's harder to defend against.

In short

The claims that cause the most damage are not the obviously false ones. They are the ones that are directionally correct but stripped of the conditions that make them true. AI can genuinely do the thing being demonstrated. What the demonstration doesn't show is the data quality required, the human review time still involved, or the gap between a controlled proof of concept and production at scale. The discipline is not skepticism; it is specificity: asking what conditions have to hold for this to be true in our environment.

There is a version of AI overclaiming that is easy to defend against. A vendor tells you AI will eliminate your entire back-office function within eighteen months. The number is invented, the timeline is fiction, and most experienced leaders will recognize it as a sales technique dressed up as a forecast.

The harder version is the claim that is mostly true.

The demonstration works. The capability is real. The case study is genuine. What is absent from the room is the set of conditions that made it work: a clean, well-structured dataset that took six months to prepare; a dedicated implementation team that is not mentioned in the slide count; a use case narrow enough to be tractable but presented as representative of the broader opportunity.

Senior leaders are not being lied to in these conversations. They are being shown a best case and implicitly invited to treat it as a typical case. That is a much more difficult thing to push back on, because the pushback sounds like skepticism about something that genuinely happened.

The discipline that protects against it is not general skepticism. It is specificity. Four questions, asked before any claim is accepted:

What were the conditions that made this work? Data quality, team composition, scope of the use case, time to value from first line of code to production deployment.

What did it cost to get to this result, fully loaded? Implementation, internal resource time, change management, ongoing maintenance.

What happens when something goes wrong? Who owns the failure, what does remediation look like, and what is the organization's exposure in the interim?

What would have to be true in our environment for this to replicate? Not in general, but specifically, given our data, our team, our existing systems, and our organizational change capacity.

None of these are hostile questions. They are the questions any senior leader would ask before a capital allocation decision in any other domain. The only reason they are not asked more often in AI conversations is that the field has been unusually good at creating an atmosphere in which asking them feels like being behind.

It is not. It is the job.
