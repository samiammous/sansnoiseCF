The Program

The Executive Brief

A full day for senior leaders who want to move from awareness to working confidence, in their own use of AI, and in how they think about it for their organizations.

Is this the right fit?

Getting the match right matters more than filling a cohort.

The program is designed for a specific kind of participant.

The right fit

Senior leaders who feel they have not yet developed a working relationship with AI. Executives who are responsible for, or will be asked to weigh in on, AI decisions at the organizational level. Groups who want to learn together, so the afternoon's discussion lands in shared context rather than individual takeaways.

Not the right fit

Technical users looking for coding, engineering, or development applications. Entry or mid-level employees, where the content is useful but the use-case focus is senior. Anyone looking for a specific product assessment or deployment strategy, which is a different engagement, and not one Sans Noise offers.

No technical background is required. The only prerequisite is a set of problems that better tools might help with.

The Program in Detail

A structured day, shaped by the group in the room.

Before the day

A short pre-work survey

Participants complete a short pre-work survey. It surfaces the areas where the group's time is best spent, so the afternoon's discussion is specific to their context rather than generic.

01Morning

Reading the landscape accurately

The day opens with the foundation most participants are missing: a clear, honest picture of how AI actually works, where it currently is and is not reliable, and how to evaluate the claims that accompany every product, pitch, and news cycle. The goal is calibration, not reassurance or alarm. Participants leave this session with a working mental model and a skepticism that is precise rather than reflexive.

02Midday

AI as a working tool

The majority of the day is hands-on. Participants work directly with AI, using real scenarios rather than hypothetical ones. The exercises cover the areas of highest leverage for senior leaders: thinking and decision support, written communications, synthesis of complex information. The session is designed to build the prompting instinct, the practical habit that determines whether AI becomes a genuine part of how someone works, or stays a novelty.

03Afternoon

Organizational judgment

The final session uses the day's direct experience as a foundation. Participants work through a framework for evaluating any AI initiative: identifying where in the business AI is most worth pursuing, how to sequence decisions, and how to hold a vendor conversation with firsthand knowledge of the technology rather than secondhand summaries. The discussion is shaped by the pre-work survey and by what surfaced during the day, so it lands in the actual context of the group.

After the day

Optional follow-up

Optional follow-up consulting is available for questions that arise as participants begin applying what they have learned to real decisions.

What a Day Looks Like

Small by design, so the room has shared stakes.

Four to eight people from the same organization means the room has shared context, shared language, and shared stakes. The exercises are worked through together. The afternoon's discussion is specific to the business in the room, not a generalized framework applied from the outside.

The facilitator is Sami Ammous, who also designed the program. There is no separation between what is taught and who teaches it.

By mid-afternoon, most groups have stopped thinking about whether AI is relevant to their work and started thinking about where to apply it first.
